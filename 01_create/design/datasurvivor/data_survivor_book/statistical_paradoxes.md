### 2. 🧠 **List of Fascinating Statistical Paradoxes**

|Paradox|One-Sentence Summary|
|---|---|
|**Simpson’s Paradox**|A trend appears in every group but reverses when data is combined, due to confounding.|
|**Berkson’s Paradox**|Conditioning on a common effect (like being admitted to hospital) can create false associations between unrelated variables.|
|**Lord’s Paradox**|Two statisticians using different methods (e.g. raw difference vs. ANCOVA) reach opposite conclusions from the same data.|
|**Suppression Effect**|Adding a variable to a regression **increases** the predictive power of another variable, even if they seemed unrelated.|
|**Ecological Fallacy**|Associations at the group level do not necessarily hold at the individual level.|
|**Reversal Paradox** (variant of Simpson's)|Adjusting for a variable flips the sign of a correlation (e.g. controlling for a third variable makes a positive correlation become negative).|
|**Monty Hall Problem**|Probabilities change after new information is revealed, but intuition often fails to update accordingly.|
|**Will Rogers Phenomenon**|Moving elements between groups can improve the average of both — like stage migration in cancer survival stats.|
|**Base Rate Fallacy**|Ignoring the underlying prevalence (base rate) leads to misinterpreting conditional probabilities.|
|**Collider Bias**|Conditioning on a variable influenced by both X and Y can create a spurious correlation between them.|
