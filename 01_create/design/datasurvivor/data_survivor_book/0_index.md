I'm writing the Data Survivor Handbook for students of biology, psyhcology, sociology, medicine and other fields who need to use lots of data but in my experiece they use it quite mindlessly. They adhere to what they were taught without understaning the underlying principles. In my experience, many look at data as a must to have forgetting what they have on the way. (Example, just today I had a consultation with a student of mine who does a clustering task on some audio data. He explained that he used PCA, but had no clue what that PCA took as an input. But he was happy about the results nevertheless.)

In my experience even if they have statistics courses at the uni, they lack general data literacy skills. So The Data Survivor Handbook (and course) focuses on filling these gaps.

The first part focuses on skills that they shold have learned but no one teaches (how to store data, various formats of data, some useful data handling tools - filtering, concatenating, etc., data visualization principles, data story telling).

The second part focuses on what they learned but with a completely wrong focus either from teachers who don't understand it themselves, or ones who do understand it very deeply but fail to pass on the information due to the curse of knowledge cognitive distortion. This would give them an undestanding of what's going on. This is the "biostatistics" or "statistics 101" or whatever you want to call it with a different spin.
